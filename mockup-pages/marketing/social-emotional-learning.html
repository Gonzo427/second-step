Social-Emotional Learning

What's SEL?

Social-Emotional Learning (SEL) is a method of teaching children the skills they need to thrive, both in the classroom and later in life. SEL teaches children techniques to gain confidence, set goals, make better decisions, collaborate with others in work and play, and navigate the world more effectively.

Committee for Children’s Second Step SEL program gives teachers an easy-to-implement, engaging way to teach SEL skills and concepts. It can help children thrive and be more successful in school—ultimately setting them up to be thoughtful and productive adults.

Let’s Be Clear

SEL isn’t kids sitting around in a circle singing songs. It’s not psychotherapy or an attempt to parent kids. SEL is also not a substitute for core academic subjects such as math, science, or literacy.

Instead, SEL concepts provide an extra dimension to education that focuses on improving cooperation, communication, and decision-making. In a world where developing emotional intelligence is critical for lifelong happiness, successful careers, and healthier relationships, SEL gives students that framework.

Research Shows SEL Benefits

Decades of research have shown the benefits of social-emotional learning. Among the findings:

In a study of 213 school-based SEL programs (PDF), SEL participants demonstrated significantly improved social and emotional skills, attitudes, behavior, and academic performance that reflected an 11-percentile point gain in overall academic achievement.

Children who are socially and emotionally competent have more friends (PDF), and more connections with positive peers, and are less likely to be rejected, isolated, and bullied. Children with friends are both happier and more successful in school.

Empathic children with good perspective-taking skills (PDF), are less likely to be physically, verbally, and indirectly aggressive toward peers.

Children’s social relations affect their feeling of connectedness at school (PDF), which affects their sense of academic competence.

Students’ social-emotional competence is an important component of effective bullying prevention. (PDF)

SEL interventions (PDF), show an average return on investment of $11 for every dollar spent.

How SEL is Taught

Children learn SEL in a variety of ways, including the behavior they see modeled by the adults in their lives. But SEL can also be taught explicitly in the classroom, in much the same way math or reading is taught:

  • The teacher explains a concept with words, pictures, video and/or audio
  • Students practice the concept with skill practice, group discussion, individual writing, or partner work
  • The teacher continues reinforcing the concept throughout the week
  • The teacher sends information home for students to work on with parents
  • The teacher checks for understanding
  • The teacher re-teaches where necessary

SEL Promotes Workforce Readiness

Increasingly, business leaders are listing emotional intelligence and collaborative skills alongside technical savvy or subject-matter mastery in their key recruitment criteria. But how exactly does social-emotional learning translate to the workplace?

Fast-paced, knowledge-based, 21st century occupations require the critical skills SEL curricula teach, including:

  • Empathy
  • Emotion management
  • Emotion recognition
  • Problem solving
  • Impulse control
  • Communication
  • Assertiveness
